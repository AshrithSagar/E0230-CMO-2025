\section*{Question 1}

Is the product of two positive definite matrices positive definite?
Why or why not?

\subsection*{Solution}

No, this is not necessarily true.
(Also, see question 10 that deals with the quadtratic form involving the product of two symmetric positive definite matrices).

\paragraph{Counter examples}
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbf{A} =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        2 & 0 \\
        0 & 1
    \end{bmatrix}
    , \quad
    \mathbf{B} =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        1 & 1 \\
        1 & 2
    \end{bmatrix}
    , \quad
    \mathbf{A} \mathbf{B} =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        2 & 2 \\
        1 & 2
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}

Both \( \mathbf{A} \) and \( \mathbf{B} \) are symmetric positive definite, but \( \mathbf{A} \mathbf{B} \) is not symmetric, although we can verify that the quadratic form \( \mathbf{x}^{\top} (\mathbf{A} \mathbf{B}) \mathbf{x} > 0 \) for all \( \mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0} \).

\subsubsection*{Product of symmetric matrices}

Note that in general, the product \( \mathbf{A} \mathbf{B} \) is not even guaranteed to be symmetric.
\( \mathbf{A} \mathbf{B} \) is symmetric, given that \( \mathbf{A} \) and \( \mathbf{B} \) are symmetric, iff \( \mathbf{A} \) and \( \mathbf{B} \) commute, i.e., \( \mathbf{A} \mathbf{B} = \mathbf{B} \mathbf{A} \).
\begin{align*}
    \mathbf{A}
    & =
    \mathbf{A}^\top,
    \qquad
    \mathbf{B}
    =
    \mathbf{B}^\top
    \\
    \implies
    {(\mathbf{A} \mathbf{B})}^\top
    & =
    \mathbf{B}^\top \mathbf{A}^\top
    =
    \mathbf{B} \mathbf{A}
    \\
    \therefore
    {(\mathbf{A} \mathbf{B})}^\top
    & =
    \mathbf{A} \mathbf{B}
    \iff
    \mathbf{A} \mathbf{B}
    =
    \mathbf{B} \mathbf{A}
\end{align*}

\paragraph{Counter examples}
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbf{A} =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        1 & 0 \\
        0 & -1
    \end{bmatrix}
    , \quad
    \mathbf{B} =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        1 & 1 \\
        1 & 1
    \end{bmatrix}
    , \quad
    \mathbf{A} \mathbf{B} =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        1 & 1 \\
        -1 & -1
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}

\( \mathbf{A} \) and \( \mathbf{B} \) are symmetric, but \( \mathbf{A} \mathbf{B} \) is not.
